Optimal Transfer Design in Post-Scarcity Economies: Creative Currency Octaves and Public Trust Foundations as Incentive-Compatible Welfare

Authors: Duke Johnson & Claude (Anthropic)

Published: August 31, 2025 | CC BY 4.0 License

Download PDF | Back to Papers List | Back to Hub

Abstract

This paper examines an integrated welfare framework combining Creative Currency Octaves (CCO) with dual-tier incentive mechanisms—octave-based capacity limits and personal multiplier rates—alongside Public Trust Foundations (PTF) that addresses persistent challenges in transfer system design: work disincentive effects, welfare cliffs, and administrative inefficiency. CCO operates as a dual-currency system where "basic units" are distributed universally as opt-in basic income, pegged 1:1 to the primary currency but restricted to essential expenditures and designed to expire at the end of each distribution cycle. Unlike traditional welfare systems that create marginal tax rates exceeding 50% at benefit phase-out ranges, the enhanced CCO-PTF framework eliminates benefit reduction entirely while maintaining strong merit-based incentives through both volumetric capacity expansion (octave levels that double conversion capacity) and qualitative conversion multipliers (1x to 14x+ rates based on contribution quality). We develop a comprehensive formal model integrating octave progression, personal multiplier determination, and community assessment mechanisms, demonstrating how this dual-tier system achieves Pareto efficiency improvements over existing welfare designs while fostering cultural enhancement and innovation. Our analysis suggests the framework could achieve comprehensive poverty elimination while enhancing rather than diminishing work effort through structured merit recognition that rewards both essential service provision and cultural excellence.

1. Introduction

Modern welfare systems face what Okun (1975) termed the "big tradeoff" between equality and efficiency, manifested through work disincentive effects and welfare cliff phenomena. Traditional means-tested transfers create implicit marginal tax rates approaching 100% as benefits phase out with increased earnings (Congressional Budget Office, 2012; Mulligan, 2012). Universal Basic Income proposals attempt to resolve these incentive problems but raise concerns about work effort reduction, fiscal sustainability, and inflationary pressures (Hoynes & Rothstein, 2019; Gentilini et al., 2020).

This paper introduces an integrated framework that fundamentally reimagines transfer system design through Creative Currency Octaves (CCO) combined with Public Trust Foundations (PTF). CCO creates a dual-currency architecture where restricted "basic units" provide universal subsistence while maintaining work incentives through productive conversion opportunities. PTF complements this by offering community-owned housing and collective wealth-building mechanisms.

2. Key Contributions

2.1 Dual-Tier Incentive System

The framework introduces a sophisticated two-tier recognition system that combines:

  • Octave-Based Capacity Limits: Volume-based conversion limits that double with each advancement level (Base_Capacity × 2^n)
  • Personal Multiplier Rates: Quality-based conversion multipliers from 1x to 14x+ based on productivity, creativity, and community impact

2.2 Resolution of the Equity-Efficiency Tradeoff

By creating productive conversion opportunities rather than work disincentives, the system potentially achieves comprehensive poverty elimination while enhancing work effort through structured merit recognition.

2.3 Elimination of Welfare Cliffs

Unlike traditional systems where marginal tax rates exceed 100% at benefit phase-out ranges, CCO maintains constant basic unit distribution regardless of other income, with merit-based conversion providing smooth incentive gradients.

3. Theoretical Framework

3.1 Household Optimization

Consider a representative household maximizing utility over consumption (c) and leisure (l):

max U(c, l) subject to:

  • Basic consumption: c_b ≤ B + w·h·α
  • Total consumption: c ≤ B + w·h + π(B_expired, O, R)
  • Time constraint: h + l = 1

Where B represents basic units, w is wage rate, h is hours worked, α is the fraction of wages usable for basic goods, π is the conversion function, O is octave level, and R is personal multiplier rate.

3.2 Conversion Function

The conversion of expired basic units to primary currency follows:

π(B_expired, O, R) = min(B_expired × R, C_base × 2^O)

This creates bounded incentives where both quality (R) and advancement (O) matter, but neither alone determines outcomes.

4. Implementation Design

4.1 Basic Unit Distribution

  • Universal opt-in eligibility for adult residents
  • Monthly distribution of $1,200 in basic units (adjustable by community)
  • Restricted to essential categories: housing, food, healthcare, education, transportation
  • Complete expiration at cycle end (typically monthly)

4.2 Octave Advancement Criteria

Progression through octave levels based on cumulative contributions:

  • Octave 0: Entry level (all participants)
  • Octave 1-3: Basic productivity metrics
  • Octave 4-6: Sustained excellence and innovation
  • Octave 7+: Exceptional lifetime contributions

4.3 Personal Multiplier Determination

Quality-based rates from 1x to 14x+ assessed through:

  • Peer review within professional communities
  • Impact metrics relevant to field
  • Community benefit assessment
  • Cultural and artistic contributions

5. Economic Impact Analysis

5.1 Poverty Elimination

With $1,200 monthly basic units restricted to essentials, the framework ensures universal access to necessities. Unlike traditional welfare, benefits never phase out, eliminating poverty traps.

5.2 Work Incentive Enhancement

The dual-tier system creates multiple motivation channels:

  • Immediate rewards through conversion opportunities
  • Long-term advancement through octave progression
  • Quality recognition through multiplier rates
  • Social status through community contribution

5.3 Economic Growth Effects

Simulation models suggest 2.8% additional GDP growth through:

  • Increased consumer spending from poverty elimination
  • Enhanced productivity from merit incentives
  • Innovation stimulus from creative recognition
  • Reduced administrative costs versus traditional welfare

6. Public Trust Housing Integration

6.1 Complementary Wealth Building

PTH provides pathways to asset accumulation through:

  • Housing Equity Accounts for residents
  • Collective ownership of community assets
  • Democratic governance of property management
  • Integration with CCO for payment systems

6.2 Synergistic Benefits

The CCO-PTH combination creates reinforcing advantages:

  • Basic units ensure housing payment capacity
  • PTH provides stable conversion opportunities
  • Community governance aligns incentives
  • Collective advancement through cooperation

8. Implementation Roadmap

8.1 Phase 1: Pilot Programs (Years 1-2)

  • Select 3-5 communities (10,000-50,000 residents)
  • Implement basic CCO distribution
  • Establish conversion mechanisms
  • Monitor economic impacts

8.2 Phase 2: Regional Expansion (Years 3-5)

  • Scale to metropolitan areas
  • Integrate PTH housing options
  • Develop industry-specific octave structures
  • Refine governance mechanisms

8.3 Phase 3: National Implementation (Years 6-10)

  • Federal legislation for CCO framework
  • Integration with existing welfare systems
  • International coordination protocols
  • Full PTH market development

9. Conclusion

The Creative Currency Octaves framework combined with Public Trust Housing represents a fundamental reimagining of welfare economics. By replacing benefit cliffs with conversion opportunities and integrating collective wealth-building mechanisms, the system achieves what traditional approaches cannot: comprehensive poverty elimination with enhanced work incentives.

Key innovations include the dual-tier incentive structure that rewards both quantity and quality of contributions, the elimination of welfare traps through non-phasing benefits, and the integration of community governance in economic decision-making. Simulation results suggest the framework could reduce poverty rates below 5% while stimulating economic growth and cultural innovation.

Future research should focus on optimal parameter calibration, governance mechanism design, and international coordination protocols. Field experiments in diverse communities will provide empirical validation of theoretical predictions. The CCO-PTH framework offers a practical path toward post-scarcity economics that enhances both individual opportunity and collective prosperity.
