Research Glossary

Comprehensive definitions for Creative Currency Octaves, Public Trust Housing, Social Zone Harmonization, Citizen Internet Portal, and related economic concepts

Acre Equity
AY-kur EK-wi-tee
PTH
A concept within the Public Trust Foundation (PTF) system that measures and distributes value based on land stewardship and productive use. The term was introduced by NotebookLM during analysis of PTF concepts, with Claude subsequently generating proposed metrics for implementation. Nations implementing PTF systems may adjust and tweak the proposed specifics based on local conditions and priorities.

Example:

The Riverside Community's Acre Equity system recognizes both the organic farmers who maintain soil health and the community garden coordinators who maximize productive food output, distributing land-based value proportionally.

Basic Units
BAY-sik YOO-nits
CCO
The foundational currency units in the Creative Currency Octaves system. Basic units are distributed universally to all community members, pegged 1:1 to the primary currency, but restricted to essential expenditures (housing, food, healthcare, education, transportation). They expire at the end of each distribution cycle to prevent accumulation and maintain economic flow.

Example:

Sarah receives 1,000 basic units monthly. She can use these for groceries, rent, and medical expenses, but not for luxury items. Any unused basic units expire at month's end.

Bias Prevention
BY-uhs pri-VEN-shuhn
Governance
Systematic safeguards within community assessment processes designed to minimize discrimination, favoritism, cultural bias, and subjective prejudice in merit recognition. Includes diverse evaluation panels, algorithmic bias detection, transparency requirements, appeal processes, and regular system auditing.

Example:

Bias prevention measures include rotating assessment panels, blind evaluation components, statistical bias monitoring, and community advocates to ensure fair treatment across all demographic groups.

Citizen Internet Portal (CIP)
SIT-uh-zuhn IN-tur-net POR-tuhl
CIP
Digital infrastructure platform that enables democratic participation, transparent governance, and community-controlled economic systems. CIP facilitates CCO operations, PTH governance, community assessment, and civic engagement through secure, user-controlled technology with privacy-by-design principles.

Example:

Through CIP, residents access their basic units, participate in community votes, view transparent budgets, engage in assessment processes, and coordinate community activities all through one integrated platform.

Collective Wealth Building
kuh-LEK-tiv WELTH BIL-ding
PTH
The systematic creation and accumulation of shared assets and economic value within a community rather than individual wealth extraction. PTH enables collective wealth building through shared ownership, community enterprises, cooperative development, and reinvestment of economic gains into community assets.

Example:

Instead of individual landlords extracting rent profits, PTH communities reinvest surpluses into improved facilities, community businesses, and resident services, building collective wealth that benefits everyone.

Community Assessment
kuh-MYOO-nuh-tee uh-SES-muhnt
Governance
The systematic evaluation process for determining octave levels and personal multiplier rates through transparent, bias-resistant mechanisms. Combines objective metrics with community feedback, peer review, and democratic input while implementing safeguards against discrimination, favoritism, and cultural bias.

Example:

The Northside CCO community uses quarterly assessment circles where residents evaluate contributions across multiple dimensions, with algorithmic bias detection and appeal processes ensuring fairness.

Compassionate Meritocracy
kuhm-PASH-uh-nit mer-i-TOK-ruh-see
Governance
A governance philosophy (also called Compassionism) that recognizes merit and achievement while ensuring basic dignity and opportunity for all. Unlike harsh meritocracy that allows failure to result in destitution, compassionate meritocracy provides universal basic security while still rewarding excellence and contribution.

Example:

Compassionate meritocracy ensures everyone has housing, healthcare, and education, while still recognizing and rewarding those who contribute exceptional value to their communities.

Conversion Capacity
kuhn-VUR-zhuhn kuh-PAS-uh-tee
CCO
The maximum number of basic units an individual can convert to unrestricted currency within a given time period, determined by their octave level. Capacity increases exponentially with octave advancement (doubling at each level), creating strong incentives for sustained community contribution.

Example:

Someone at Octave 2 has a conversion capacity of 2,000 basic units monthly, while Octave 4 provides 8,000 unit capacity, rewarding consistent long-term community engagement.

Conversion System
kuhn-VUR-zhuhn SIS-tuhm
CCO
The mechanism by which community members convert expired basic units into unrestricted primary currency through productive activities. Conversion rates depend on both octave level (capacity) and personal multiplier rates (quality). Activities include essential services, creative work, skill development, community building, and cultural contribution.

Example:

Marcus, at Octave 3 with a 4x multiplier, can convert up to 4,000 basic units monthly by teaching guitar lessons, participating in community gardens, and mentoring youth.

Creative Currency Octaves (CCO)
kree-AY-tiv KUR-uhn-see OK-tavs
CCO
A dual-currency economic system that provides universal basic income through restricted 'basic units' while maintaining strong work incentives through a productive conversion system. Named for the octave-like doubling pattern of conversion capacities across advancement levels. CCO addresses welfare cliff problems by never reducing benefits while rewarding productive contribution through enhanced conversion opportunities.

Example:

The Riverside Community implemented CCO, providing every resident with basic units for essentials while creating conversion opportunities through community service, artistic creation, and skill development.

Cultural Value Integration
KUL-chuhr-uhl VAL-yoo in-tuh-GRAY-shuhn
CCO
The recognition and economic incorporation of artistic, creative, and cultural contributions within the CCO system. Unlike traditional economies that often undervalue cultural work, CCO explicitly rewards artistic excellence, cultural preservation, creative innovation, and community cultural development through enhanced multiplier rates.

Example:

The local poetry circle, traditional craftsmen, and community theater group all receive high multiplier rates for their cultural contributions, making artistic work economically viable within the CCO framework.

Democratic Governance
dem-uh-KRAT-ik GUV-ur-nuhnts
Governance
Participatory decision-making structures within PTH communities where residents have meaningful input into community policies, resource allocation, development priorities, and conflict resolution. Democratic governance ensures community self-determination while maintaining accountability and inclusive representation.

Example:

The Westside PTH community holds monthly assemblies where residents vote on budget priorities, housing policies, and community rules, with working groups handling detailed implementation.

Dual-Tier Incentives
DOO-uhl TIER in-SEN-tivz
CCO
The sophisticated two-dimensional recognition system in CCO that combines volumetric capacity expansion (octave levels) with qualitative conversion enhancement (personal multiplier rates). This design rewards both consistent participation and exceptional quality, creating incentives for sustained community engagement while recognizing diverse forms of excellence.

Example:

The dual-tier system allows both steady contributors (high octave, moderate multiplier) and exceptional artists (moderate octave, high multiplier) to achieve significant economic success through different pathways.

Essential Expenditures
uh-SEN-shuhl ik-SPEN-duh-churz
CCO
Categories of spending where basic units can be used, typically including housing, food, healthcare, education, transportation, basic utilities, and other necessities for dignified living. The definition is designed to cover fundamental needs while excluding luxury or speculative purchases that could create problematic market dynamics.

Example:

Essential expenditures include rent, groceries, medical care, school supplies, and public transit, but exclude luxury cars, jewelry, or speculative investments.

Expiration Cycle
ek-spuh-RAY-shuhn SY-kuhl
CCO
The time period after which unused basic units expire and are removed from circulation. Typically monthly or quarterly, expiration prevents hoarding, maintains economic flow, ensures currency reflects current productive capacity, and prevents accumulation-based inequality within the basic units system.

Example:

Basic units expire monthly, encouraging regular economic participation and preventing individuals from accumulating large stores of restricted currency that could distort the system.

Housing Equity Accounts
HOW-zing EK-wi-tee uh-KOWTS
PTH
Individual accounts that track each PTH resident's accumulated equity stake in the community's collective assets. Unlike traditional homeownership where equity is tied to specific property values, Housing Equity Accounts represent shared ownership in the entire community's growing wealth, including improved facilities, business development, and appreciation.

Example:

After 5 years in PTH, Carlos has built $45,000 in his Housing Equity Account through rent payments and community contributions, giving him a proportional stake in the community's $12 million in collective assets.

Incentive Compatibility
in-SEN-tiv kuhm-PAT-uh-bil-uh-tee
Economics
Economic design principle where individual rational behavior aligns with desired collective outcomes. CCO achieves incentive compatibility by making productive contribution the path to greater economic opportunity, ensuring that pursuing personal benefit also benefits the community. The system encourages broad participation while recognizing diverse contributions.

Example:

In CCO, the best way to maximize personal income is through high-quality community contribution, creating incentive compatibility between individual success and collective wellbeing.

Merit Recognition
MER-it rek-uhg-NISH-uhn
Governance
The systematic acknowledgment and reward of valuable contributions to community wellbeing across diverse domains including essential services, creative work, innovation, mentorship, cultural preservation, and community building. Merit recognition in CCO avoids traditional meritocracy problems by recognizing multiple forms of value and preventing accumulation of unjust advantages.

Example:

Merit recognition equally values the night-shift janitor maintaining community health, the teacher developing innovative curricula, and the artist creating inspiring murals - all contributing essential value in different ways.

Octave Levels
OK-tav LEV-uhls
CCO
Progressive advancement tiers in the CCO system that determine conversion capacity levels. Each octave level doubles the previous level's conversion capacity (Base_Capacity Ã— 2^n). Advancement is based on consistency of contribution, community engagement, skill development, and long-term commitment to community wellbeing. Named after musical octaves due to the doubling frequency pattern.

Example:

Octave 1 allows 1,000 basic unit conversions monthly, Octave 2 allows 2,000, Octave 3 allows 4,000, etc. Advanced practitioners might reach Octave 6+ with conversion capacities of 32,000+ basic units monthly.

Personal Multiplier Rates
PUR-suh-nuhl MUL-tuh-ply-ur RAYTS
CCO
Quality-based conversion multipliers ranging from 1x to 14x+ that determine the value of each converted basic unit. Higher multipliers are earned through exceptional quality, innovation, cultural contribution, and positive community impact. Combined with octave levels, they create the dual-tier incentive system that rewards both consistency and excellence.

Example:

Lisa has a 6x multiplier for her innovative teaching methods and community leadership. When she converts basic units through educational activities, each unit becomes worth 6x the base value.

Post-Scarcity Economics
POHST-SKAR-suh-tee ek-uh-NOM-iks
Economics
Economic frameworks designed for conditions where basic human needs can be met abundantly for all, allowing focus to shift from survival to creativity, relationships, and self-actualization. CCO and PTH represent transition mechanisms toward post-scarcity by ensuring basic security while incentivizing innovation and cultural development.

Example:

As automation reduces the cost of basic goods, post-scarcity economics focuses on how to distribute abundance fairly while maintaining incentives for human flourishing and continued innovation.

Productive Activities
pruh-DUK-tiv ak-TIV-uh-teez
CCO
Diverse forms of valuable contribution that enable basic unit conversion, including essential services, creative work, education, healthcare, childcare, eldercare, environmental stewardship, skill development, community building, cultural activities, and innovation. The definition is intentionally broad to recognize multiple forms of social value creation.

Example:

Productive activities range from traditional employment to community gardening, from artistic creation to peer tutoring, from childcare to organizing neighborhood events - all recognized as valuable community contributions.

Public Trust Housing (PTH)
PUB-lik TRUST HOW-zing
PTH
A community-owned housing model that creates pathways for collective wealth building while providing stable, affordable housing. Properties are held in public trust and owned collectively by residents through democratic governance structures. PTH integrates housing with services like childcare, healthcare, education, and skill development, while residents build equity in collective assets rather than individual property.

Example:

The Riverside Trust Housing community of 200 units operates democratically, with residents voting on improvements and sharing in the community's growing asset value through their Housing Equity Accounts.

Restricted Currency
ri-STRIK-tuhd KUR-uhn-see
CCO
Currency units that can only be spent on predetermined categories of essential goods and services such as housing, food, healthcare, education, and transportation. In CCO, basic units function as restricted currency designed to mitigate inflationary pressure in non-essential markets while ensuring universal access to necessities.

Example:

Restricted currency prevents housing speculation and luxury inflation while ensuring everyone can afford basic needs. It's like food stamps, but expanded to cover all essential categories of spending.

Social Zone Harmonization (SZH)
SOH-shuhl ZOHN har-muh-nuh-ZAY-shuhn
SZH
A comprehensive framework for organizing communities and regions to optimize social, economic, and environmental outcomes through coordinated planning and resource allocation. SZH works to balance diverse community needs while maintaining local autonomy and democratic governance through voluntary inter-municipal cooperation.

Example:

The Greater Metro SZH initiative coordinates transportation, shared services, and environmental management across twelve municipalities while preserving each community's local character and decision-making authority.
